What are Pullbacks and the Psychology Behind Pullbacks?

A pullback is a price movement that moves in against the trend. It is a temporary price movement before it resumes back into the main market direction. Pullbacks are sometimes referred to as price Correction or retracement. Pullback occurs when the price moves at least one bar against the opposite direction of the trend.

  1. The hope of comparison is to find the top or bottom of weakness in price move by the novice.
  2. If the pullback is sluggish, contra traders will lose hope, while bullish traders will regain confidence.
  3. If the pullback down is strong and signals bearish conviction, the bar will get more aggressive, and the bulls will start to doubt their position.

Benefits of Pullback Trading Strategy

There are several benefits of the Pullback Trading Strategy. Some of them are as follows:

 
  1. Trading pullback lets you have a tighter stop loss as your trade location is good, and this gives you a better risk to reward
  2. From a psychological standpoint, it’s easier to pull the trigger as you’re buying high and selling low

PULLBACK Trading Strategy TYPES

These corrective moves either are the time or price correction, but they denote a change in the order flow and participation depending upon the types of trend. There are TWO TYPES of Pullback Trading Strategy. They are as follows:

  1. TIME CORRECTION
  2. PRICE CORRECTION

A strong trend: (Time Correction)

In strong trending markets, you’ll have pullbacks that usually stock moves in a horizontal, low volatility trendless manner. Because the pullback is shallow, it isn’t easy to time your entry on a pullback. Instead, you can trade the breakout or find an entry on the lower timeframe.

 

Healthy trend: (PRICE CORRECTION)

A healthy trend is between a strong and a weak trend. You can expect a pullback towards the SR level.

Weak trend (TRENDING RANGE TYPE):

In weak trending markets, you’ll have steeper pullbacks that usually retrace toward major Support and Resistance

Complex Pullbacks

Complex pullbacks happen when the price steps into a consolidating phase in the form of any pattern. It then remains consolidated for a while before it resumes the trend. No one really knows how long it remains consolidated before it moves again. Generally forming a continuous pattern like

  1. Rising / Falling Wedge Pullback
  2. Rising / Falling Flag Pullback
  3. Pennants Pullback
  4. Widening Wedge Pullback

Pullback Trading Strategy Summary:

A pullback in trading refers to a temporary reversal of an asset’s price from its prevailing trend. A pullback trading strategy involves identifying and acting on these pullbacks within the context of a larger trend. It is based on the premise that most trends will experience periods of consolidation or retracement before continuing in the original direction. Traders use pullbacks to enter the market at a better price than if they had entered during the trend’s peak.

 

Here’s how a basic pullback trading strategy might look:

Identify the Trend:

First and foremost, you need to identify the prevailing trend. This can be done through:

  • Moving Averages: A price above a longer-term moving average (like the 50-day or 200-day) suggests an uptrend, while a price below it suggests a downtrend.
  • Trend Lines: Drawing a line that connects the lows in an uptrend or the highs in a downtrend can help visualize the trend.
  • Higher Highs and Lows: In an uptrend, look for a pattern of higher highs and higher lows. In a downtrend, look for lower lows and lower highs.

Find the Pullback:

Once the trend is identified, look for a pullback within that trend. A pullback can be identified through the:

  • Price Action: Look for the price to move against the trend and then stabilize or show signs of resuming the prior trend.
  • Retracement Levels: Use Fibonacci retracement levels to identify potential reversal points.
  • Support and Resistance: In an uptrend, old resistance levels can become new support, and vice versa for a downtrend.

Entry Points:

After identifying a pullback, you need to find a suitable entry point:

  • Bounce: Enter the trade when the price starts bouncing back in the direction of the trend.
  • Confirmation: Wait for confirmation from other indicators (like moving averages, RSI, stochastic, etc.) that the pullback is ending and the trend is resuming.

Set Stop Losses:

A key part of the strategy is to limit risk with stop losses:

  • Below Support/ Above Resistance: Place a stop loss just below the new support level in an uptrend or above the new resistance level in a downtrend.
  • Percentage of ATR: Alternatively, use a set percentage of the price or the Average True Range (ATR) to determine where to set your stop loss.

Take Profits:

Determine in advance where to take profits:

  • Previous Highs/Lows: You might target previous highs in an uptrend or lows in a downtrend.
  • Risk-Reward Ratio: Use a risk-reward ratio to set your profit target. For example, if you’re risking $1 per share, look for a move that could potentially make $2 or $3 per share.

Considerations for Pullback Strategies:

  • Volume: Look for changes in volume; a decrease in volume on the pullback and an increase on the resume of the trend can be a good sign.
  • Avoid Choppy Markets: Pullback strategies work best in markets with clear trends, not sideways or choppy markets.
  • Market Context: Always consider the broader market sentiment and economic indicators.
  • Multiple Timeframes: Analyzing the pullback on multiple timeframes can provide a better perspective and increase the reliability of the strategy.

Like all trading strategies, a pullback strategy requires practice, patience, and strict risk management. Also, it’s important to note that sometimes, what appears to be a pullback could actually be the start of a trend reversal. Hence, traders should be prepared for scenarios where the trend does not resume as expected.
