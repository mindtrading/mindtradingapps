Why Swing Trade?

Swing trading is trying to capture upswings and downswings in stock prices. Swing Trading is a strategy that focuses on small profits in short-term trends (momentum trading). The profit might be smaller, but if done consistently over time, they can compound into excellent returns. Swing Trading positions are usually held for one day to a few weeks.

There are various types of entry systems to take swing trade, like

 
  1. Price action with Pivot breakout (unique)
  2. Supply and demand trading
  3. Support and resistance trading
  4. Any indicator-based (moving average/macd /rsi etc.)

What are Pullbacks and the Psychology behind Pullback?

A pullback is a price movement that moves in against the CURRENT trend. It is a temporary price movement before it resumes in the main direction. Pullbacks are sometimes referred to as price Correction or retracement.

 
  1. The hope of comparison is to find the top or bottom of weakness in price move by the novice.
  2. If the pullback is sluggish, contra traders will lose hope, while bullish traders will regain confidence.

Benefits of Pullback Trading Strategy

There are several benefits of the Pullback Trading Strategy. Some of them are as follows:

  1. Trading pullback lets you have a tighter stop loss as your trade location is good, and this gives you a better risk to reward
  2. From a psychological standpoint, it’s easier to pull the trigger as you’re buying high and selling low

VCP with Pivot Point Breakout:

First of all, what is VCP (volatility contraction pattern)? The price moves from a period of low volatility to high volatility

  1. After a strong impulse move (volatility increased)
  2. Then, the price goes sideways (volatility contracted). If you want to enter trades during a low-volatility period. it gives favorable risk-to-reward on trades, and stop-loss tends to be SMALLER

The basic characteristics of the pattern are that stocks in long-term up trends take rest (pause and consolidate) as some holders exit their positions, and the stock is accumulated again by buyers in the market. The VCP chart pattern can provide opportunities for STRONG breakouts and be used across any time frame.

Some VCPs are

  1. Triangles
  2. Ranges [Rectangles]
  3. Base
  4. Cup & Handle

Swing Trading Strategy Using Pivot Point Summary:

Swing trading using pivot points is a strategy that involves taking advantage of short-term price movements, often around key levels determined by pivot points. Pivot points are technical indicators derived from the previous trading session’s high, low, and close and are used to identify potential support and resistance levels. Here’s a basic strategy outline for swing trading with pivot points:

1. Calculate Pivot Points:

Start by calculating the pivot points for the asset you’re trading. The standard pivot point (PP) is calculated as:


From the PP, you can calculate additional support and resistance levels (S1, S2, R1, R2, and so on) using various formulas.

 

2. Identify the Market Trend

  • Use longer time frames to determine the overall market trend. Is the market trending up, down, or in a range?
  • Pivot points are particularly useful in range-bound markets but can also help identify reversal points in trending markets.

3. Determine Entry Points

  • For a Bullish Swing: Look for prices that are holding above the PP. If the price retraces to the PP or the first support level (S1) and shows signs of support (like a bullish candlestick pattern), consider this an entry point for a long position.
  • For a Bearish Swing: Look for prices that are holding below the PP. If the price retraces to the PP or the first resistance level (R1) and shows signs of resistance (like a bearish candlestick pattern), consider this an entry point for a short position.

4. Set Stop Losses

  • Place a stop loss just below the support level for long positions or above the resistance level for short positions. The idea is if the price breaks through these levels, the pivot point prediction is incorrect.

5. Establish Profit Targets

  • Profit targets can be set at the next level of pivot point resistance (for long positions) or support (for short positions).
  • Some traders use a risk-reward ratio to set targets, aiming for a larger reward than the risk taken with the stop loss.

6. Monitor the Trade and Adjust

  • Adjust your stop loss to lock in profits or reduce risk as the price moves.
  • Be aware of how the price reacts to pivot point levels throughout the day, as these can become new entry or exit points.

7. Incorporate Other Indicators

  • To increase the strategy’s effectiveness, use other technical indicators or tools, such as moving averages, RSI, MACD, or Bollinger Bands, to confirm pivot point signals.
  • Volume can also be a good confirmation; increasing volume at a pivot point level can strengthen the signal.

8. Risk Management

  • As with any trading strategy, manage risk by allocating only a small percentage of your portfolio to any trade.
  • Be mindful of economic events or news releases that could cause volatility and affect your trades.

Pivot points are dynamic and change every trading session, reflecting the new price action. This makes them ideal for swing traders looking to capture short-term moves. However, they should not be used in isolation. A confluence of signals from other indicators provides a more robust basis for entering and exiting trades. Always backtest any new strategy with historical data before applying it to live markets to understand its potential efficacy and risk.
