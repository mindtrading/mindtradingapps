Why Volume Matters in Trading?

  • Market Interest price move on large volume shows something might have triggered the move. This could be news or earnings fundamental change, or economic releases such as the non-farm payrolls or an interest rate decision.
  • Momentum- Rising volume with price increasing suggests bullish strength increasing. Falling volume with price increases suggests that momentum is waning, and there could be a reversal. For more details, please check our Volume Price Action Analysis article.
  • Confirmation – Traders also use volume to confirm chart patterns breakout or breakdown any breakout or breakdown for more details about the breakout trading strategy.
  • Climax- In a strongly established trend, large price and volume spikes often precede a major reversal: the security has either been substantially overbought or oversold, and market professionals step in and buy/sell against the trend. 
  • Spike within trading range- In a trading range, large volumes on a wide-ranging day will signal the likely direction of a breakout.

Volume Spike Trading Strategy Summary:

A volume spike trading strategy involves identifying significant increases in trading volume that are substantially above the average trading volume. This increase often signifies that large institutional investors are taking positions and can lead to either a continuation of the current trend or a reversal. Here’s how a volume spike strategy might work in various market scenarios:

1. Identifying Volume Spikes
First, you would identify what constitutes a “spike” in the context of the traded asset. This can vary depending on the market and the asset. Still, a common benchmark might be a volume that’s, for example, 2 to 3 times greater than the average volume over a specified period.

2. Assessing the Context
It’s crucial to understand the context in which a volume spike occurs. Is the market in a trend or trading sideways? Is there recent news that might be affecting the asset? A volume spike without context may not provide a reliable signal.

3. Watching Price Action
Once a volume spike is identified, the next step is to look at price action. A volume spike accompanied by a significant price move can indicate the move’s strength.

Bullish Scenario:

  • If a volume spike occurs during an uptrend and the price breaks above resistance, it may be a signal to go long or buy.
  • In a downtrend or at a support level, a volume spike could signal a potential reversal to the upside.

Bearish Scenario:

  • During a downtrend, a volume spike with a price drop through support could signal continuation, and a short position might be considered.
  • Conversely, in an uptrend, a spike in volume coupled with a significant price drop could indicate a reversal to the downside.

4. Confirmation with Indicators
Traders often seek confirmation from other technical indicators before acting on a volume spike. For instance, they might use the Relative Strength Index (RSI) to confirm overbought or oversold conditions or Moving Averages to determine the strength of a trend.

5. Entry and Exit Points
A trading strategy based on volume spikes should have clear rules for entry and exit points. Traders might enter a trade after a volume spike confirms a breakout from a pattern and exit when volume declines significantly, or price action indicates a reversal.

 

6. Stop-Loss Orders
Given the volatility of volume spikes, setting a stop-loss order is important to manage risk. The stop-loss could be based on a percentage of the entry price, a fixed dollar amount, or technical levels like recent highs or lows.

Example Strategy:

  • Identification: Look for a volume at least double the average of the previous five days.
  • Price Action: Confirm the price is breaking out of a consolidation pattern with the volume spike.
  • Confirmation: Wait for the close of the candle to ensure it’s not a false breakout.
  • Entry Point: Enter the trade at the opening of the next candle after the breakout candle.
  • Exit Point: Set a profit target at a previous level of resistance/support or use a trailing stop.
  • Stop-Loss: Place a stop-loss just below the breakout level or under a significant technical level.

Considerations:

  • False Signals: Volume spikes can sometimes be misleading, especially when they are not supported by price action.
  • Market News: A volume spike might be a reaction to news, which can be unpredictable. It’s crucial to differentiate between volume spikes due to technical factors and those due to fundamental news.
  • Liquidity: In thinly traded stocks or during off-market hours, volume spikes might be anomalies and not true indicators of market sentiment.

Volume spike strategies often work best when combined with other forms of analysis. Always backtest a strategy using historical data before applying it to live trading, and consider the overall market conditions and the specific characteristics of the asset being traded.
