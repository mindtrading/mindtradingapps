9 Powerful Must Known Profitable Candle Stick Pattern

There are many candlesticks, but only nine of them are essential for you to know. Why? Because they are extremely powerful and well-liked, why waste time on the others?
These candlesticks are excellent trade entry indications when they appear at support and resistance levels or Fibonacci levels.

1. The Doji Candlestick Pattern is the first: The doji candlestick patterns are made up of just one (single) candlestick. As seen below, there are 4 different varieties of doji candlesticks:

A. Depending on where it appears, the DOJI CROSS can be seen as either a bullish or bearish signal
B. When the GRAVESTONE DOJI forms in an uptrend or at a resistance level, it is regarded as a bearish reversal candlestick.
C. The DRAGONFLY DOJI candlestick pattern is seen as bullish when it appears during a decline or at a support level.
D. The LONG-LEGGED DOJi represents a period of indecision on the part of both bulls and bears, and it can be interpreted as either a bearish or a bullish signal depending on where it occurs (uptrend/resistance level=bearish signal, downtrend/support level=bullish signal).
2. Engulfing candlestick patterns are number two.

Two candlestick patterns form the engulfing patterns. In a bullish engulfing pattern, the initial candle will be bearish, followed by a second candle that is extremely bullish and that fully engulfs the previous candle.

A = Bullish Engulfing.
B = Bearish Engulfing

● A bullish engulfing can indicate that a downtrend may be coming to an end when it forms near a downtrend or support level.
● A bearish engulfing is a hint that an uptrend may be coming to an end when it forms during an uptrend or near a resistance level.
3. Harami Candlestick Patterns

Two candlestick patterns form the engulfing patterns.

Bullish Harami
This is a pattern made up of two candles. A extremely bearish candlestick forms the first candlestick, which is followed by a short, bullish candle that is entirely obscured by the previous candle's shadow. This will be your bullish (buy) indication if you observe it during a decline or in a zone of support.

Bearish Harami
This pattern may be a bearish reversal signal that the uptrend is ending and that you should go short when it appears at a resistance level or during an uptrend (sell). It is also an opposite of bullish harami,

A = Bullish Harami
B = Bearish Harami

4. Candlestick pattern covered in dark clouds

The dark cloud is made up of 2 candlestick and it is a bearish reversal candlestick pattern. When the second candle forms, it tells a completely different story. Its bearish and it closes at around the same point as the first candlestick. The first candlestick is a bullish one, displaying a strong upward momentum.

Dark cloud cover candlestick patterns are a bearish reversal indication, and you should consider going short (sell) when you observe them in an uptrend or at a level of resistance.

5. Candlestick Pattern With Piercing Line

Dark cloud cover is the opposite of the piercing line. This could be forming at a support level or during a downturn. The first candlestick offers a very gloomy story, but when the second candle forms, it provides a highly bullish story. This indicates that the bulls are strengthening and that the bears are losing momentum, which could lead to a rise in the market price.

The second bullish candlestick should end up near where the previous candlestick's mind-point was. Consider going long (buying) if you notice the piercing line pattern emerging at support levels or in a market that is downtrending because this could be a bullish reversal signal.

6. Shooting Star Pattern

Given that they are so simple to identify on any chart, this is one of the most trustworthy candlesticks as well as one of the most widely used.

The shooting star is a single candlestick pattern that indicates a bearish reversal when it appears during an uptrend or at a level of resistance. As a result, you should aim to sell if it does so.

The shooting star is most time refereed to as the Bearish Hammer, Inverse Hammer Bearish Pin Bar or Inverted Hammer. They are all similar to the shooting star.

7. Pattern Of The Hammer Candlestick

The hammer candlestick is a single candlestick design that is the reverse of the shooting star candlestick pattern and is seen as a bullish reversal candlestick pattern.
It has a short or no upper wick and a very long tail.

You should pay attention when it forms in a downturn or near support levels. Considering that this is a very likely bullish reversal candlestick pattern, you should be observing to buy long.

9. The Railway Tracks Pattern

There are bearish and bullish railway track candlestick patterns and it’s a twocandlestick pattern. One distinguishing quality of this pattern is that they resemble parallel railroad tracks and the candlesticks should practically be mirror images of one another in terms of length and body.

● For a bearish railway track, the initial candle is bullish and is followed by a second candlestick with almost precisely the same length and body. This indicates that bears have gained dominance and bulls are losing ground. Therefore, you should look to sell when you see the bearish railway track pattern in an uptrend or in a zone of resistance because it may be a hint that the downtrend is about to begin.

● The bullish railway track layout is analogous but in the other direction. Take note if you observe this in a downtrend or a support area it may be a sign that the market is about to move upward and your signal to purchase.

10. The Spinning Top Pattern

Spinning tops can be either reversal or continuation candlestick formations. Small bodies and longer upper and lower shadows are characteristics of spinning tops. Spinning tops represent uncertainty. A single candlestick pattern called a spinning top can be either bullish or bearish.

When you observe a bearish spinning top in a support region or in a downtrend, this can be interpreted as bullish reversal signal if the high of the bearish spinning top pattern is broken by the upside
Similar to this, when the low is broken to the downside, a bullish spinning top pattern in an uptrend or at a resistance level might be interpreted as a bearish signal.

When compared to other candlesticks, spinning tops are noticeably shorter in length, and their body length is a few steps wider than that of doji candlesticks. The wicks on both sides of a spinning top should be almost the same length, which is another important feature. The formation of spinning tops on support or resistance levels simply indicates to me that the market's bulls and bears are unsure of where to push the price, and the move in that direction is usually indicated when the next candle that forms breaks through the spinning top's low or high!
